PROJECT 2: WEBSITE PROPOSAL

Hybrid Arts Studio — Illustration Portfolio Website

Principles of Web Design

1. Client and Topic

This website will be a portfolio of my character illustration and digital artwork. As both the client and the designer, I am creating this site to showcase my original characters and illustrations I have developed over years of independent work as a digital artist. The portfolio will serve as a public-facing creative identity and professional point of contact under the brand name Hybrid Arts Studio.


2. Development Process and Engagement

Because I am both the designer and the client for this project, the development process will be guided by thorough personal research rather than external client meetings. I will study what makes illustration portfolios effective for independent artists, with particular focus on how working artists in the character design, comics, and concept art spaces present their work online.

Specifically, my research will include:

  • Analyzing the portfolio sites of independent illustrators and comic artists both on personal domains and platforms like Behance, ArtStation, and Cargo to identify what organizational patterns, visual hierarchies, and navigation styles are most effective for artwork-forward content.
  • Researching the "OS desktop UI" aesthetic in web design, including existing examples of browser-based desktop simulations, to understand how to incorporate it into HTML and CSS without sacrificing usability or accessibility.
  • Reviewing UX principles for portfolio sites: how visitors browse, what they expect to find, how quickly they decide whether to engage further, and what friction points most commonly cause them to leave.
  • Gathering feedback from peers in creative communities (such as art Discord servers and forums) on what they look for when visiting an artist's portfolio site.

This research will directly shape the structure, content priorities, and interactive design decisions made throughout the development process.


3. Testing

Before launch, the website will undergo multiple rounds of testing to ensure it functions correctly, meets its own stated goals, and provides a consistent experience across different environments.

Testing will include the following:

  • Functional testing of all interactive elements: the desktop UI navigation system (opening and closing "windows", taskbar interaction), gallery lightbox views, hover effects, and any CSS animations or transitions used throughout the site.
  • Content completeness review: confirming that all artwork thumbnails link to the correct full-size gallery views, that all artist information and text is present and accurate, and that no placeholder content remains live on any page.
  • Navigation testing: walking through every user path on the site, from the home screen to opening a gallery window to viewing individual artwork, to ensure no dead links, broken flows, or confusing dead ends exist.
  • Responsive/layout testing: verifying that the site renders correctly at common desktop screen resolutions, since the desktop UI concept is intentionally designed for larger screens. Appropriate messaging or a graceful fallback will be implemented and tested for mobile visitors.
  • Performance check: reviewing image file sizes to ensure pages load within an acceptable timeframe even on slower connections.

Browser testing will be conducted in the following browsers:

  • Microsoft Edge (primary development browser)
  • Mozilla Firefox
  • Google Chrome

Each browser will be checked for visual consistency, functional interactivity, and correct font and color rendering.


4. Description

About the Business

Hybrid Arts Studio is a freelance creative brand run by me, a U.S. Army veteran, UI/UX designer, and self-taught digital artist. The studio serves as the umbrella identity for my original illustration work and comic development and is separate from my design projects. The "business" at this stage is a personal creative practice and more like freelance work, not a registered business entity, but it operates with a professional mindset.

The goal of Hybrid Arts Studio is to give my creative work a dedicated home on the web that reflects my aesthetic, showcases my illustration work, and positions me as a professional artist to potential collaborators, commissioners, and employers in the creative industry.

Purpose of the Website

The Hybrid Arts Studio portfolio site exists to present my character illustration and digital artwork in a way that is visually distinctive, easy to browse, and memorable. Rather than relying on third-party platforms like ArtStation or Instagram—which impose visual templates and compete for attention with other creators—this site gives me full control over presentation and branding. The site functions as both a gallery and a creative statement: the design itself communicates who I am as an artist.

Target Audience

The intended audience for this site spans several overlapping groups, all of whom share an interest in original character art, independent comics, and niche creative communities:

  • Independent comic readers and webcomic enthusiasts (ages 16–35) who actively seek out original stories and characters from indie creators. This group tends to be digitally native, spends significant time on platforms like Webtoon, Tumblr, Twitter/X, and Discord, and values authenticity and personal artistic voice over polished corporate aesthetics. They are likely to access the site on desktop, though mobile should be accounted for.
  • Art commissioners (ages 18–40), including tabletop roleplayers, writers, and fans who hire independent artists to create character illustrations. This group is results-focused and wants to quickly assess an artist's style, skill level, and range. They may arrive via social media links or word of mouth.
  • Creative industry peers and potential collaborators (ages 20–40), including other illustrators, comic artists, game designers, and UI/UX or graphic design professionals who are evaluating my work for possible collaboration or professional referral. This group tends to be detail-oriented and sensitive to design quality—a poorly designed portfolio site will undercut their confidence in the artist.
  • Recruiters or hiring managers in creative or tech-adjacent fields (ages 28–50), particularly those looking for designers with a demonstrated visual sensibility and personal brand. This group values professionalism and ease of navigation above all.

Most visitors will arrive on desktop or laptop devices, either from social media profile links or search engines. The desktop-UI concept is intentionally designed for this context. The audience skews younger (teens to mid-thirties), is comfortable with unconventional web interfaces, and appreciates when a creator's site reflects a distinct personality rather than following a generic template.

Impact of Target Audience on Development

Knowing my audience means the site must balance two priorities: personality and professionalism. I cannot put off potential recruiters, but it also cannot be so safe and generic that it fails to leave an impression on the comic readers and art commissioners it most needs to engage. The desktop UI concept accomplishes this nicely. It is fun and expressive while still being organized and navigable. The "windows" structure means content is always just a click away, and the visual grammar of a desktop environment is familiar to almost everyone.

Knowing the audience accesses the site primarily on desktop informs the layout breakpoints, the decision to center the experience on a widescreen canvas, but I also plan to make it responsive. Accessibility, like readable font sizes, sufficient color contrast, and keyboard navigation, will also be addressed throughout development.

Original Content

All content on the website will be created by me, including:

  • All character illustrations and digital artwork featured in the gallery are original pieces created by me in Clip Studio Paint.
  • All written copy: the artist bio, artwork descriptions, and any about-page text.
  • All UI design elements: the desktop wallpaper, window styling, taskbar, and overall visual design.
  • Any animated GIF assets used as decorative elements within the desktop UI theme.

External Content Sources

The following external resources may be used to support the site:

  • Web fonts sourced from Google Fonts (open license).
  • Icons, some decorative elements.
  • CSS reset or base stylesheets as a development starting point.
  • Any JavaScript libraries used for lightbox gallery functionality or smooth scroll behavior.

Content Update Frequency

New artwork will be added to the gallery continuously, depending on creative output. Currently, because of classes, I haven't finished many works. The artist bio and contact information will be updated as needed, roughly a few times per year. The overall site design and structure are intended to be the same once launched, with major redesigns considered annually or when the portfolio's scope changes significantly.


5. Growth and Maintenance

Over time, the Hybrid Arts Studio portfolio site is expected to grow in two primary dimensions: the volume of artwork in the gallery and the scope of creative work it represents. As my comic, Full Moon Rising, develops, a dedicated section for comic pages and world information may be added.

The site could also eventually expand to include a commissions page (currently using VGen for this), a store for prints or digital downloads, and a blog or development diary for behind-the-scenes content.

To make ongoing maintenance easier during development, I can:

  • Store artwork in a consistent, organized folder structure with standardized file naming, making it easy to add new images without restructuring the gallery.
  • Use CSS custom properties (variables) for all color and typography values so that any future visual updates can be made in one place.
  • Write my HTML semantically and with reusable patterns, making it straightforward to add new gallery sections or pages without rewriting layout logic from scratch. Like a manually maintained CMS?
  • Use GitHub for version control, providing both a backup and a deployment pipeline through GitHub Pages.

Maintenance tasks and their frequency will include:

  • Adding new artwork to the gallery as new pieces are completed.
  • Updating the artist bio and contact information as needed.
  • Checking all links and interactive features for functionality.
  • Reviewing the site on updated browser versions for compatibility.
  • General design review and potential refresh.

6. Organization

Content Organization and Navigation

The site's organizing concept is a simulated PC desktop environment. When a visitor lands on the site, they see a stylized desktop—a wallpaper (original artwork), a taskbar at the bottom, and desktop icons or a start-style menu for navigating to different sections. Each section of the site opens in a draggable "window" overlaid on the desktop canvas, mimicking the experience of working in a windowed operating system.

This metaphor creates a natural and intuitive organizational structure: each "window" is its own content area, and the desktop itself is always present as the home base. Navigation is primarily through the taskbar and desktop icons, supplemented by window title bars that allow closing and minimizing.

Pages and Sub-Topics

The site will include the following main content areas, each opening as a desktop window:

  • index.html: the initial landing page serving as a desktop home screen (hub) with custom background wallpaper
  • gallery.html: Gallery window serving as primary portfolio section. Thumbnail grid with thumbnails opening into a full-screen lightbox view with a dark or light background and left/right arrow navigation between pieces.
  • characters.html: page for meeting my characters, like "Meet the Team" but my OCs.
  • lore.html: Worldbuilding/lore page
  • process.html: Art process/behind-the-scenes window
  • commissions.html: Commission info/status window
  • about.html: Artist bio window
  • faq.html: Frequently asked questions
  • contact.html: Contact window with links to socials; third-party contact form.
  • credits.html: Acknowledges any external libraries, fonts, or tools used in building the site.

Page Layout and Navigation

The primary navigation lives on the "desktop" as folders and things like contacts and credits will be in the taskbar at the bottom or top of the page, similar to a real desktop OS. Clicking a desktop folder or taskbar icon opens the corresponding window. Windows can be closed using an X button in their title bar. The animated page doll character sits in the corner of the desktop. It might function as a back-to-top button when clicked.

Inside the gallery window, images will be arranged in a grid of image thumbnails and, once in lightbox, navigated using arrow keys and an X or Escape to close.

Wireframes

Wireframes for the home/desktop screen and one interior page (the Gallery window) will be created in Figma since I already use it for personal works. These wireframes document the layout of the taskbar, desktop icon placement, window chrome structure, and the thumbnail grid layout used inside the gallery window.

Site Map / Flow Chart

A site map was developed showing the relationship between the desktop hub and each window/page. The flow is non-linear by design: any window can be opened from the desktop at any time, and multiple windows can theoretically be open simultaneously. The site map illustrates this hub-and-spoke model, with the desktop at the center and each content window radiating outward. The site map is included as a supplemental attachment to this proposal.


7. Security

This site will not include an e-commerce component at launch. No shopping cart, payment processing, or transaction handling will be present. As a result, many of the most critical security vulnerabilities associated with financial data do not apply.

The site will not have password-protected content. All portfolio content is intended to be publicly accessible. The purpose of the site is maximum visibility for the artwork, not restricted access. There is no members-only area, no client login portal, and no subscription-gated content.

The only potential user input will be through a contact form, if one is implemented. In that case, the form will be handled through a secure third-party service to avoid exposing a backend server or email address directly.

As the designer and developer, specific steps I can take to help secure the site include:

  • Using HTTPS exclusively—GitHub Pages provides free SSL/TLS certificates, ensuring all traffic is encrypted in transit.
  • Not embedding any user-generated content or third-party comment systems that could introduce injection risks.
  • Keeping all external dependencies (JavaScript libraries, fonts) sourced from reputable CDNs and reviewed for known vulnerabilities before inclusion.
  • Not publishing any sensitive personal information (home address, phone number) in the site's source code or visible content.

8.Web Hosting

Hosting Selection Criteria

I consider the following when selecting my hosting service:

  • Cost and scalability: For a personal portfolio at launch, a free or low-cost hosting solution is appropriate. The host should offer a clear upgrade path if the site's traffic or complexity grows.
  • Reliability and uptime: The host should have a strong reputation for uptime (ideally 99.9% or better), ensuring the portfolio is always accessible to visitors, collaborators, or potential clients.
  • Performance: The host should support fast load times. For a static site serving images, this means efficient delivery of assets—ideally with CDN support.
  • Custom domain support: The host must support mapping a custom domain name (hybridarts.studio) so the site does not live on a subdomain that looks unprofessional.
  • HTTPS/SSL support: The host must provide free SSL certificates to ensure the site loads securely, which is both a trust signal to visitors and a factor in search engine ranking.
  • Ease of deployment: Since the site will be version-controlled in GitHub, a host that integrates directly with GitHub for continuous deployment is strongly preferred.
  • Static site support: Because this site is built in HTML, CSS, and JavaScript with no server-side language required, a static site host is ideal and more efficient than a general-purpose PHP or database-backed host.

Selected Hosting Service

I have selected GitHub Pages as the hosting platform for this project. It is free, integrates directly with the GitHub repository used for version control, provides automatic HTTPS via SSL, supports custom domain names, and is well-suited to static HTML/CSS/JS sites. It meets every criterion identified above with no cost barrier at this stage.

Special Technologies

The following technologies will be used:

  • SSL/HTTPS: Required for security and professionalism. Provided automatically by GitHub Pages.
  • A JavaScript lightbox library: Used for the full-screen gallery viewer. No server-side processing required; runs entirely in the browser.
  • CSS animations and transitions: Used for the desktop UI interactivity and nav animations. No special server support needed.

Programming and Scripting Languages

The site will be built with HTML5, CSS3, and vanilla JavaScript. No server-side language (PHP, Python, Node.js) is required, as the site is entirely static. GitHub Pages supports static file hosting natively, making it a direct match for this technology stack. If a contact form is added, it will use a third-party service so that no backend scripting is needed on the host.

Domain Name

The selected domain name is hybridarts.studio. This name was chosen because it has my brand identity (Hybrid Arts) and uses the .studio, which is appropriate and recognizable for creative professionals. It is clear, memorable, and easy to spell. It is distinct from generic portfolio domain patterns and functions as a branding statement in itself. Availability was confirmed via Porkbun and Namecheap domain searches.


9. Marketing

Social media platforms will be the primary marketing channels for the Hybrid Arts Studio portfolio site where visual art communities are active: Bluesky, Instagram, and Tumblr. Posts featuring new artwork will include a link back to the portfolio site, driving traffic from an existing or growing follower base. The site URL will be included in all social media profile bios and any forum or community profiles where I participate.

From an SEO perspective, the following elements will be addressed during development:

  • Descriptive, keyword-relevant page titles and meta description tags for every page.
  • Semantic HTML structure (proper use of header, main, section, article, and figure elements) to ensure search engines can correctly parse the site's content.
  • Alt text on all images, describing the artwork in terms a search engine can index and that also serve accessibility purposes.
  • A consistent and descriptive URL structure (e.g., hybridarts.studio/gallery/) that reinforces the site's content for indexing purposes.
  • A robots.txt file and sitemap.xml to guide search engine crawlers.
  • Fast page load times through optimized image formats (WebP).

10. Project Reflection

For the wireframes required in this proposal, I used Figma as my design tool of choice rather than the suggested options of Justinmind or Framebox. I already use Figma through my UI/UX design work and personal web design work. Figma's component and frame system make it ideal for wireframing the desktop UI concept, since I could mock up multiple "window" states and the taskbar layout efficiently and at varying fidelity levels. Figma is also very intuitive, from my experience, even as a new UI/UX and web designer.

Building the flowchart diagram for the site was extremely helpful for thinking through the non-linear structure of this project's navigation model. Unlike a traditional linear site where pages flow in a hierarchy from home to subpages, the desktop UI concept uses a hub-and-spoke model where the desktop is always the central reference point and any window can be opened from any state. Mapping this out made me realize I needed to account for the fact that multiple windows might be open at the same time. This influenced my thinking about z-indexing, window focus behavior, and how the taskbar would need to reflect active windows, or not, because that's fine too.

I used Figma for the site diagram as well, using its connector and shape tools to build the flowchart rather than a dedicated diagramming app. This kept everything in one tool, which simplified the workflow. Dedicated diagramming tools would have worked just as well and might be preferable for students who want a lower learning curve for this project.

The main challenge in developing the navigation concept for this project was reconciling the fake desktop UI with web accessibility and usability expectations. Traditional desktop OS navigation relies heavily on mouse interaction—clicking, dragging, and right-clicking—which does not translate naturally to keyboard navigation or screen readers. Resolving this meant thinking carefully about how to ensure keyboard accessibility: each "window" trigger would need to be a focusable, actionable element; windows would need to keep the focus accordingly when open. I also had to consider how to handle the experience on mobile devices, where the desktop metaphor breaks down entirely, but it can still work. My resolution is to stack desktop icons like a normal responsive grid and design each window (page) to stack on top of one another like an ideal mobile responsive grid. Dragging the windows, of course, will be disabled in mobile.

For someone new to creating web pages, I think the most common challenge in a project like this would be the gap between a strong visual concept and the technical skills needed to implement it. It is easy to sketch an ambitious design, for example, this simulated desktop with draggable windows, and then discover that the HTML and CSS required to build it are significantly more complex than anticipated. Managing that gap requires breaking the project down into smaller, achievable technical milestones and being willing to simplify or adjust the design when the implementation proves too complex for the current skill level. Starting with the structural HTML and basic CSS before adding any interactivity is a discipline that pays off throughout the development process. I have the basic knowledge to achieve this project. I'm still new to JS, but I feel that this is a doable project for me. If anything, good JS practice!
